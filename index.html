Modern Philosophy

Rationalism · France · 1596 – 1650

Descartes' Works

The founder of modern philosophy and father of analytic geometry. Descartes demolished scholastic tradition through radical doubt and rebuilt all knowledge on a single foundation: the thinking self.

Rationalism · Netherlands · 1632 – 1677

Spinoza's Works

The most radical philosopher of the seventeenth century. Excommunicated, banned, and denounced as the prince of atheists — Spinoza identified God with Nature itself and demonstrated human freedom through the geometric method of Euclid.

Rationalism · Germany · 1646 – 1716

Leibniz's Works

The last universal genius — mathematician, logician, diplomat, theologian, and the most systematically ambitious philosopher of the modern period. Leibniz built a world of windowless monads, each mirroring the universe from its own point of view, held in perfect harmony by a God who chose the best of all possible worlds.

Empiricism · England · 1632–1704

Locke's Works

The founder of British empiricism and liberal political philosophy — whose Essay remade epistemology, whose Two Treatises founded constitutional democracy, and whose Letter on Toleration established the separation of church and state.

Immaterialism · Ireland · 1685–1753

Berkeley's Works

The Irish bishop who denied the existence of matter — and meant it. Berkeley's central claim that to be is to be perceived, that the physical world consists entirely of ideas in minds and has no existence independent of perception, was the most audacious thesis in the history of philosophy. He defended it with arguments of such clarity, force, and charm that Samuel Johnson's famous stone-kick refutation missed the point entirely; Berkeley had anticipated and answered every such objection before Johnson was born.

Scottish Enlightenment · Edinburgh · 1711–1776

Hume's Works

The most thorough sceptic in the Western tradition — who demolished the rational foundations of causation, personal identity, and natural religion, and built in their place a philosophy of human nature grounded in custom, sentiment, and the limits of reason. He died calmly and without religious consolation, the closest thing the Enlightenment produced to a Socratic death.

Transcendental Idealism · Prussia · 1724–1804

Kant's Works

The philosopher who drew the map of reason's territory — and marked its borders. Kant never travelled more than fifty miles from Königsberg, yet the revolution he accomplished in philosophy was more far-reaching than any journey could have produced. His three Critiques constitute the most ambitious and systematic philosophical project since Aristotle: a complete inventory of the powers and limits of human reason in its theoretical, moral, and aesthetic employment. His neighbours set their clocks by his daily walk. His last words were reported as “Es ist gut” — it is good.

German Idealism · Saxony · 1762 – 1814

Fichte's Works

The philosopher who made the self the foundation of all reality — and paid the price for it. Fichte took Kant’s critical philosophy and drove it to conclusions that terrified Kant himself. The entire world, he argued, is the product of the absolute I — an infinite, self-positing activity of consciousness that creates the not-I as the necessary resistance against which freedom, morality, and selfhood become possible. Accused of atheism in 1799, driven from Jena, he reinvented himself in Berlin as a philosopher of national destiny. He remains the most underrated titan of modern philosophy — the bridge between Kant and Hegel.

German Idealism · Württemberg · 1775 – 1854

Schelling's Works

The philosopher of nature, freedom, and the dark ground of existence — the most protean thinker in the German Idealist tradition. Where Fichte reduced nature to a mere obstacle for the moral will, Schelling restored it to philosophical dignity: nature is visible Spirit, Spirit is invisible nature. His 1809 Freiheitsschrift confronted the reality of evil, the dark ground in God, and the abyss of freedom with a depth that neither Fichte nor Hegel ever matched. His career spanned six decades and at least four distinct philosophical systems — each responding to problems left unresolved by the last.

German Idealism · Stuttgart · 1770 – 1831

Hegel's Works

The philosopher who claimed to bring the history of philosophy to its completion — and came closer than anyone before or since. Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel constructed the most comprehensive philosophical system in the Western tradition: a dialectical logic in which every concept generates its own negation and is reconciled in a higher synthesis, a phenomenology in which consciousness ascends through successive stages to absolute knowledge, a philosophy of right in which freedom is realised in the institutions of family, civil society, and the state, and a philosophy of history in which Spirit (Geist) progressively comes to know itself through the cultures and civilisations of the world. His influence is incalculable: Marx, Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Heidegger, Sartre, and virtually every major thinker of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries defined themselves for or against him. He died of cholera in Berlin on 14 November 1831, at the height of his fame.

Post-Kantian Philosophy · Danzig · 1788 – 1860

Schopenhauer's Works

The philosopher of the will, of suffering, and of the aesthetic redemption of existence. Arthur Schopenhauer argued that the world as we experience it is mere representation — a veil of appearances structured by the mind’s own forms — and that behind this veil lies the thing-in-itself: a blind, purposeless, ceaselessly striving Will that is the inner nature of everything that exists. Human life is a pendulum swinging between suffering and boredom; the only genuine escapes are aesthetic contemplation, moral compassion, and the ascetic denial of the will-to-live. Ignored for three decades after the publication of his masterwork, he became the most widely read philosopher in Europe after the publication of Parerga and Paralipomena in 1851. His influence on Nietzsche, Wagner, Wittgenstein, Freud, Tolstoy, Mann, Beckett, and Borges is incalculable. He died in Frankfurt on 21 September 1860, alone but finally famous.

Nihilism & Revaluation · Germany · 1844–1900

Nietzsche's Works

The philosopher with a hammer — who declared the death of God, diagnosed the nihilism of modern civilisation, and demanded a revaluation of all values. Nietzsche wrote ten major works in twelve years before a mental collapse at forty-four ended his productive life, but his influence on every subsequent philosophical, literary, and cultural movement has been incalculable. He died in 1900 having never recovered consciousness of what he had accomplished.

Existentialism · Copenhagen · 1813–1855

Kierkegaard's Works

The father of existentialism — who dismantled Hegel’s system from within, insisted that existence cannot be reduced to thought, and spent his entire literary career asking one question: what does it mean to be a single individual before God? He died at forty-two, exhausted by his attack on the Danish State Church, refusing communion from an official pastor, convinced he had been used by Providence as a corrective.

Historical Materialism · Germany & England · 1818–1895

Marx & Engels

The two thinkers who turned Hegel right-side up, placed philosophy in the service of revolution, and produced the most consequential body of social thought in modern history. Marx spent thirty years in the British Museum working out the hidden logic of capital; Engels managed a Manchester cotton factory to fund the work. Together they wrote the document that changed the world: a spectre is haunting Europe.

Classical Athens

The Academy

Founded by Plato around 387 BC near Athens, the Academy was the Western world's first institution of higher learning. It operated continuously for nearly 900 years until its closure by Emperor Justinian I in 529 AD.

The Lyceum

Founded by Aristotle in 335 BC, the Lyceum was a center of empirical research. Its members, known as the Peripatetics, studied everything from biology to the constitutions of 158 Greek city-states.
