Timeline

The lives of Plato and Aristotle set against the political, military, and intellectual events that shaped the world in which they thought.

The Archaic Background
c. 776–500 BCE
c. 776
First Olympic Games

Traditional date of the first Olympiad at Elis, the primary chronological reference point for Greek historical reckoning.

Historical
c. 750
Homer composes the Iliad and Odyssey

The foundational texts of Greek culture. Plato calls Homer “the educator of Greece” before subjecting his work to philosophical critique in the Republic.

Historical
c. 624
Birth of Thales of Miletus

The first natural philosopher. Aristotle regards him as the founder of the inquiry into nature (Metaphysics 983b). Proposed water as the arche.

Philosophy
c. 610
Birth of Anaximander

Proposed the apeiron (the boundless) as the first principle. Authored the first known work of Western philosophy. Produced the first map of the known world.

Philosophy
585
Thales predicts a solar eclipse

According to Herodotus, Thales predicted the eclipse that halted the battle between the Lydians and the Medes. The earliest dateable event in Greek intellectual history.

Historical
c. 570
Birth of Pythagoras

Born on Samos. Founded the philosophical brotherhood at Croton. Number as the principle of all things. His influence on Plato was immense.

Philosophy
c. 535
Birth of Heraclitus

Born in Ephesus. All things flow (panta rhei). The Logos as the rational principle governing the cosmos. The young Plato studied under his follower Cratylus.

Philosophy
c. 515
Birth of Parmenides

Born in Elea. Being is one, eternal, and unchanging. The most formidable challenge in Pre-Socratic philosophy. Every subsequent thinker had to answer him.

Philosophy
508/7
Cleisthenes establishes Athenian democracy

The constitutional reforms that created the democratic institutions within which Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle would live, teach, and think.

Historical
The Classical Age
c. 500–323 BCE
490
Battle of Marathon

Athens defeats the Persian invasion force of Darius I. The victory secures Athenian independence and inaugurates the classical period of Athenian confidence and cultural production.

Historical
480
Battles of Thermopylae and Salamis

The Greek alliance repels Xerxes’ massive invasion. Athens is sacked and burned but the Persian fleet is destroyed at Salamis. The defining moment of Greek collective identity.

Historical
c. 470
Birth of Socrates

Born in Athens, son of the stonemason Sophroniscus and the midwife Phaenarete. Shifted philosophy from cosmology to the examination of human life and virtue. Wrote nothing.

Philosophy
c. 460
Birth of Democritus

Born in Abdera. Together with Leucippus, developed atomism: the universe consists of indivisible particles moving through infinite void. The most prolific author of antiquity.

Philosophy
461–429
Age of Pericles

Pericles dominates Athenian politics. The Parthenon is built. Athens becomes the intellectual and artistic center of the Greek world. Anaxagoras, Protagoras, and other thinkers gather in the city.

Historical
431–404
The Peloponnesian War

Athens and Sparta devastate the Greek world in a generation of conflict. Plato is born during the war. It ends with Athens’ defeat, the installation of the Thirty Tyrants, and a crisis of Athenian self-confidence from which Platonic philosophy partly emerges.

Historical
c. 428
Birth of Plato

Born in Athens into one of the most distinguished aristocratic families. His mother Perictione traced her lineage to Solon. The Peloponnesian War is in its third year.

Plato
423
Aristophanes’ Clouds performed

The comedy portrays Socrates as a buffoonish natural philosopher running a “Thinkery.” Plato’s Apology has Socrates cite the play as contributing to the prejudice against him.

Historical
c. 408
Plato meets Socrates

Around the age of twenty, Plato encounters Socrates—a meeting that, by his own account, redirected the entire course of his life. He reportedly burned his tragedies.

Plato
404
Fall of Athens — The Thirty Tyrants

Athens surrenders to Sparta. The oligarchic regime of the Thirty, which included Plato’s relatives Critias and Charmides, rules by terror for eight months. The experience decisively shapes Plato’s distrust of both democracy and tyranny.

Historical
399
Trial and execution of Socrates

Convicted of impiety and corrupting the youth by a jury of 501. Refuses to flee. Drinks the hemlock. The founding event of the Western philosophical tradition. Plato is 29. He departs for Megara.

Philosophy
c. 399–387
Plato’s travels

After Socrates’ death, Plato travels to Megara (studying with Euclides), reportedly to Cyrene (meeting the mathematician Theodorus), and to southern Italy and Sicily (encountering the Pythagorean circle of Archytas). First Sicilian visit ends in disaster; Plato reportedly sold into slavery.

Plato
c. 387
Plato founds the Academy

Established in a grove sacred to the hero Academus, roughly a mile northwest of Athens. The first permanent institution of higher learning in the Western world. “Let no one ignorant of geometry enter.” It will endure for over three centuries.

Plato
384
Birth of Aristotle

Born in Stagira on the Chalcidice peninsula. His father Nicomachus serves as physician to King Amyntas III of Macedon. Orphaned young.

Aristotle
367
Aristotle enters the Academy

At the age of seventeen, Aristotle enrolls in Plato’s Academy. He will remain for twenty years. Plato reportedly calls him “the mind of the school” and “the reader.”

Aristotle
367
Plato’s second Sicilian voyage

Travels to Syracuse at Dion’s invitation to educate the young tyrant Dionysius II. The attempt to realize the Republic’s philosopher-ruler ideal in practice. Ends in failure and personal danger.

Plato
361
Plato’s third Sicilian voyage

Returns to Syracuse one final time. Again ends disastrously. The experience may have tempered the Republic’s idealism with the pragmatic political philosophy of the Laws.

Plato
359
Philip II becomes King of Macedon

Begins the military and political transformation of Macedon from a marginal kingdom into the dominant power in Greece. Father of Alexander. His rise will reshape the world in which both the Academy and the Lyceum operate.

Historical
c. 348/347
Death of Plato

Dies in Athens at approximately eighty, reportedly while attending a wedding feast. His nephew Speusippus succeeds him as head of the Academy. The Laws, his last work, is left unfinished. Aristotle departs Athens shortly after.

Plato
347–335
Aristotle’s years away from Athens

Travels to Assos (studying with Hermias, marrying Pythias), then to Mytilene on Lesbos (biological fieldwork with Theophrastus). In 343, summoned by Philip II to tutor the thirteen-year-old Alexander. Prepares an annotated Iliad for his pupil.

Aristotle
338
Battle of Chaeronea

Philip II defeats the combined forces of Athens and Thebes. The eighteen-year-old Alexander commands the Macedonian left wing. The age of the independent Greek polis is effectively over.

Historical
335
Aristotle founds the Lyceum

Returns to Athens and establishes his school in a gymnasium dedicated to Apollo Lykeios. Its members are called Peripatetics. The Lyceum organizes systematic research across every field of knowledge. The first research institute.

Aristotle
334–323
Alexander’s conquests

Alexander conquers the Persian Empire, reaching Egypt, Mesopotamia, Central Asia, and the borders of India. He founds cities bearing his name across three continents. The Hellenistic world is born. Aristotle reportedly receives biological specimens from the campaign.

Historical
323
Death of Alexander — Aristotle flees Athens

Alexander dies in Babylon at thirty-two. Anti-Macedonian sentiment surges in Athens. Aristotle withdraws to Chalcis, reportedly saying he will not allow Athens to “sin twice against philosophy.”

Aristotle
322
Death of Aristotle

Dies in Chalcis on the island of Euboea at the age of sixty-two. Theophrastus succeeds him as head of the Lyceum. His published dialogues will gradually be lost; his esoteric treatises will survive to become the most influential body of philosophical writing in history.

Aristotle
The Hellenistic World
c. 323–31 BCE
c. 306
Epicurus founds the Garden

Establishes his school in Athens, admitting women and slaves. Atomism, the swerve, and ataraxia. “Where death is, I am not.” The third great philosophical institution of Athens.

Philosophy
c. 300
Zeno of Citium founds the Stoa

Begins teaching at the Painted Colonnade (Stoa Poikile) in the Athenian agora. Logic, physics, and ethics as an integrated system. Living in agreement with nature. Stoicism will become the dominant philosophy of the Hellenistic and Roman world.

Philosophy
c. 268
Arcesilaus turns the Academy skeptical

Becomes scholarch and inaugurates the systematic suspension of judgment (epoche). Claims to recover the authentic spirit of Socrates against Stoic dogmatism. The beginning of the “Middle Academy.”

Philosophy
155
The philosophical embassy to Rome

Carneades (Academic), Critolaus (Peripatetic), and Diogenes of Babylon (Stoic) are sent to Rome. Carneades speaks for and against justice on consecutive days. Cato demands their expulsion. Greek philosophy enters the Roman world.

Philosophy
c. 100
Apellicon recovers Aristotle’s manuscripts

Purchases the deteriorated manuscripts from the heirs of Neleus at Scepsis and brings them to Athens. The texts had rotted in a cellar for nearly two centuries.

Philosophy
86
Sulla sacks Athens

The Roman general Sulla besieges and captures Athens. The grove of Academus is destroyed. Sulla seizes Apellicon’s library, including the Aristotelian manuscripts, as war spoil and transports it to Rome.

Historical
c. 60
Andronicus of Rhodes publishes the Aristotelian corpus

Arranges the treatises in the systematic order still used today. Places the works on first philosophy “after the Physics”—coining the term “metaphysics.” Creates the Aristotle that all subsequent ages have read.

Philosophy
The Roman & Late Antique Period
c. 31 BCE–529 CE
c. 50 CE
Birth of Epictetus

Born a slave in Hierapolis. “Some things are within our power, while others are not.” His Enchiridion becomes foundational for cognitive behavioral therapy two millennia later.

Philosophy
c. 200
Alexander of Aphrodisias — the Commentator

Holds the imperial chair of Peripatetic philosophy in Athens. His commentaries on Aristotle become the benchmark. His reading of De Anima III.5 shapes a millennium of debate.

Philosophy
c. 244
Plotinus begins teaching in Rome

Founds his philosophical circle. The Enneads reinterpret Plato as emanation from the One. The last great original philosophical system of antiquity. Neoplatonism will shape Augustine, Islamic philosophy, and the entire medieval Christian tradition.

Philosophy
c. 270
Porphyry composes the Isagoge

His introduction to Aristotle’s Categories becomes the most widely read logical text for a millennium. His deferred questions about universals catalyze the longest debate in the history of philosophy.

Philosophy
410
Sack of Rome by the Visigoths

Alaric’s Visigoths sack Rome. Augustine begins composing The City of God, profoundly shaped by Neoplatonic philosophy, in response to the crisis.

Historical
485
Death of Proclus

The last great pagan philosopher. His Elements of Theology and Platonic commentaries represent the summit of Neoplatonism. His systematic influence will shape Islamic and Christian theology for a millennium.

Philosophy
529
Justinian closes the philosophical schools in Athens

The Emperor Justinian orders the closure of the Neoplatonic Academy, the last institutional descendant of Plato’s original foundation. Simplicius and other philosophers reportedly travel to the Sasanian court in Persia. The continuous tradition of Greek philosophical teaching in Athens, stretching back over nine centuries to Plato, comes to an end.

Philosophy
The Transmission & Recovery
c. 750–1499 CE
c. 830
The House of Wisdom in Baghdad

Under the caliph al-Ma’mun, the Bayt al-Hikma undertakes the systematic translation of the Greek philosophical and scientific corpus into Arabic. Hunayn ibn Ishaq and his circle produce the standard Arabic Aristotle. The most ambitious translation project in human history.

Philosophy
c. 1037
Death of Avicenna

The most original Islamic philosopher. His distinction between essence and existence transforms Aristotelian metaphysics and is adopted by Aquinas. His Canon of Medicine serves as the standard medical textbook until the seventeenth century.

Philosophy
c. 1150
The Toledo translators begin rendering Aristotle into Latin

Gerard of Cremona and other scholars translate the Physics, De Anima, Metaphysics, and other works from Arabic into Latin. Aristotle arrives in the medieval Latin West together with the commentaries of Avicenna and Averroes.

Philosophy
1198
Death of Averroes

The greatest Aristotelian commentator since Alexander. The Latin scholastics call him simply Commentator. His doctrine of the unity of the intellect provokes the fiercest controversy in thirteenth-century philosophy.

Philosophy
1210
Aristotle banned at the University of Paris

The teaching of Aristotle’s natural philosophy is prohibited. The ban is renewed in 1215. By 1255, the entire Aristotelian corpus is required reading. From prohibition to curriculum in forty-five years.

Historical
1274
Death of Thomas Aquinas

Dies at forty-nine, leaving the Summa Theologiae unfinished. “All that I have written seems like straw.” The greatest synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology. His work is declared the official philosophy of the Catholic Church in 1879.

Philosophy
1417
Poggio Bracciolini rediscovers Lucretius

The Italian humanist finds a manuscript of De Rerum Natura in a German monastery. Lost for over a thousand years, the Epicurean poem re-enters Western culture. Stephen Greenblatt has argued it helped lay the conceptual foundations of modernity.

Philosophy
1438–39
Gemistos Plethon lectures in Florence

The Byzantine philosopher arrives for the Council of Ferrara-Florence. His lectures on the superiority of Plato over Aristotle electrify the Italian intellectual elite. He directly inspires Cosimo de’ Medici to establish a new Platonic Academy.

Philosophy
1462–69
Marsilio Ficino translates the complete Plato into Latin

The first integral translation of all thirty-six dialogues. Plato becomes fully available to Western readers for the first time in over a thousand years. Ficino coins the term “Platonic love” (amor platonicus). His translations remain standard until the nineteenth century.

Philosophy
1486
Pico della Mirandola composes the 900 Theses

At twenty-three, Pico draws on every philosophical and theological tradition and offers to defend all 900 theses in public disputation. The Pope suppresses the event. The prefatory Oration on the Dignity of Man becomes the manifesto of Renaissance humanism.
