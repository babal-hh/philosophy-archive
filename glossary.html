Glossary of Terms

The essential vocabulary of ancient Greek philosophy—the terms in which Plato and Aristotle thought, argued, and constructed their understanding of reality.

A
Agathon (to Agathon) “The Good”

The supreme principle in Plato’s metaphysics. In the Republic (505a–509b), the Form of the Good occupies the highest position in the hierarchy of Forms, analogous to the sun in the visible world: as the sun provides both the light by which objects are seen and the energy by which they grow, the Good provides both the intelligibility by which the Forms are known and the being by which they exist. It is thus “beyond being” (epekeina tes ousias) in dignity and power—a formulation that profoundly influenced Plotinus’s conception of the One. In Aristotle, agathon is not a single transcendent Form but is “said in many ways” (Nicomachean Ethics 1096a); the practical good for human beings is eudaimonia, activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.

Associated: Plato, Republic VI–VII; Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics I; Plotinus, Enneads V–VI
Aisthesis “Sense-perception”

The faculty and act of sense-perception. For Plato, aisthesis grasps only the changing, particular world of becoming and can never yield genuine knowledge (episteme), which is reserved for the intellect’s apprehension of the eternal Forms. In the Theaetetus (151d–186e), Plato systematically refutes the identification of knowledge with perception. For Aristotle, by contrast, aisthesis is the indispensable starting point of all knowledge: “All men by nature desire to know. An indication of this is the delight we take in our senses” (Metaphysics 980a). Sensation receives the form of the perceived object without its matter, as wax receives the impression of a signet ring (De Anima 424a). The Stoics held that all impressions (phantasiai) derive from aisthesis, with the “cataleptic impression” providing the criterion of truth. The Epicureans went further, maintaining that all sensations are true; error arises only in the judgment applied to them.

Associated: Plato, Theaetetus; Aristotle, De Anima II–III; Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus
Aitia (Aition) “Cause,” “explanation”

Aristotle’s doctrine of the four causes (Physics 194b–195a) is one of the most influential explanatory frameworks in the history of philosophy. To fully explain any natural object or process, one must identify: (1) the material cause (hyle)—the matter out of which something is made (the bronze of a statue); (2) the formal cause (eidos, morphe)—the form, pattern, or definition that makes it what it is (the shape of the statue); (3) the efficient cause (to hothen he kinesis)—the agent or source of change that brings it into being (the sculptor); and (4) the final cause (telos)—the end, purpose, or “that for the sake of which” (the statue’s function as a dedication or ornament). Aristotle criticized his predecessors for recognizing only some of these causes: the Milesians identified only material causes, the Pythagoreans formal causes, Empedocles efficient causes. The final cause is, for Aristotle, primary: nature acts for an end, and teleological explanation is the deepest form of understanding.

Associated: Aristotle, Physics II; Metaphysics I, V
Aletheia “Truth,” “unconcealment”

Literally “un-forgetting” or “un-concealment” (from a-, “not,” and lethe, “forgetting, concealment”). In Parmenides’ poem, the goddess guides the philosopher along the “Way of Truth” (hodos aletheies), which reveals that Being is one, unchanging, and complete. For Plato, aletheia is what the philosopher grasps when the soul ascends from the shadowy world of appearance to the sunlit realm of the Forms; the Allegory of the Cave (Republic 514a–520a) dramatizes this ascent from darkness (doxa) to light (aletheia). For Aristotle, truth consists in saying of what is that it is, and of what is not that it is not (Metaphysics 1011b); this “correspondence” conception became foundational for Western epistemology. In the twentieth century, Heidegger reinterpreted aletheia as the primordial “unconcealment” of Being, prior to and more fundamental than propositional truth—a reading that, though controversial among classicists, profoundly influenced continental philosophy.

Associated: Parmenides, DK 28 B1–B8; Plato, Republic VII; Aristotle, Metaphysics IV
Anamnesis “Recollection”

Plato’s doctrine that all learning is recollection of knowledge the soul possessed before its incarnation in a body. In the Meno (81a–86b), Socrates demonstrates this by eliciting geometrical knowledge from an uneducated slave boy through questioning alone, without teaching him anything—the boy “recovers” the knowledge from within himself. In the Phaedo (72e–77a), the argument is extended: our ability to recognize imperfect sensible instances as imperfect (two sticks that are “approximately equal” but not perfectly so) presupposes prior acquaintance with the Form of Equality itself, which we must have known before birth. The doctrine serves multiple philosophical functions: it resolves Meno’s paradox (how can you search for what you do not know?), it provides evidence for the soul’s pre-existence and immortality, and it establishes the epistemological priority of the Forms over sensible particulars. Aristotle rejected anamnesis, arguing that knowledge arises through abstraction from sensory experience (Posterior Analytics II.19).

Associated: Plato, Meno, Phaedo, Phaedrus
Apeiron “The boundless,” “the indefinite”

Anaximander’s proposed arche—the first principle from which all things arise and into which all things dissolve. Unlike Thales’ water or Anaximenes’ air, the apeiron is not any determinate substance but something prior to all qualitative determination: boundless in extent, indefinite in character, and eternal in duration. Aristotle interprets it as that which has no limit or boundary (peras), and discusses it extensively in the Physics (III.4–8), where he distinguishes between the potential infinite (which he accepts: a line can always be further divided) and the actual infinite (which he rejects: no actually infinite magnitude or number exists). The apeiron also appears in Pythagorean philosophy as one member of the fundamental pair of limiters (perainonta) and unlimiteds (apeira) that Philolaus identified as the constituents of all things.

Associated: Anaximander, DK 12 B1; Aristotle, Physics III; Philolaus
Aporia “Perplexity,” “impasse”

Literally “without passage” or “without way through”—the state of genuine intellectual puzzlement that results when confident assumptions are shown to be inconsistent. In the Socratic dialogues, aporia is the characteristic outcome of the elenchus: the interlocutor who began with a confident definition of justice, courage, or piety discovers that his beliefs are contradictory, and is left in a state of productive bewilderment. Far from being a failure, aporia is for Socrates the necessary precondition of genuine philosophical inquiry—it clears away false certainty and opens the soul to authentic understanding. Aristotle preserves the term in a somewhat different sense: in the Metaphysics (III.1, 995a24–995b4), he systematically catalogues the aporiai (puzzles, difficulties) that any adequate metaphysical theory must resolve, treating them as the starting point of philosophical investigation rather than its terminus.

Associated: Plato, Meno, Laches, Euthyphro; Aristotle, Metaphysics III
Arche “Beginning,” “principle,” “first cause”

The fundamental principle, origin, or source from which all things derive. The search for the arche defines the Milesian project: Thales proposed water, Anaximander the apeiron, Anaximenes air. In Aristotle’s reconstruction (Metaphysics I), the history of Pre-Socratic philosophy is essentially the history of successive proposals for the arche. For Aristotle himself, an arche is that from which something first comes to be, or through which something is first known, or by which something is first governed (Metaphysics 1013a). The term carries a double sense of temporal origin and logical ground: the arche is both that from which things historically arose and that which makes them rationally intelligible. In Neoplatonism, the arche becomes the One, the ultimate principle that is the source of all being but is itself beyond all predication.

Associated: Thales, Anaximander, Anaximenes; Aristotle, Metaphysics I, V; Plotinus
Arete “Virtue,” “excellence”

The excellence or optimal functioning of any thing according to its nature. A knife has arete when it cuts well; a horse when it runs well; a human being when it lives well. The term carries no exclusively moral connotation in Greek; it encompasses any form of excellence, from athletic prowess to intellectual brilliance to moral integrity. The Homeric heroes possess arete as martial valor and noble bearing. The Sophists claimed to teach arete as civic and rhetorical competence. Socrates asked the revolutionary question: what is arete as such, and can it be taught? He identified it with knowledge: no one who truly understands the good would willingly choose evil. Plato elaborated this into the doctrine of the four cardinal virtues: wisdom (sophia), courage (andreia), temperance (sophrosyne), and justice (dikaiosyne), each corresponding to a part of the soul and a class in the ideal city. Aristotle distinguished intellectual virtues (acquired through teaching) from moral virtues (acquired through habituation, ethismos), and defined moral arete as a stable disposition (hexis) to choose the mean between excess and deficiency relative to the agent and the situation (Nicomachean Ethics 1106b–1107a).

Associated: Homer; the Sophists; Socrates; Plato, Republic IV; Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics II
Ataraxia “Tranquility,” “freedom from disturbance”

The undisturbed condition of the soul, free from anxiety, fear, and emotional turbulence. Ataraxia is the highest goal of Epicurean and Pyrrhonian ethics, though achieved by very different means. For Epicurus, ataraxia results from the rational elimination of the fears that torment the soul: the fear of the gods (who exist but take no interest in human affairs), the fear of death (“where death is, I am not”), and the fear of unattainable desires. For the Pyrrhonian skeptic, ataraxia follows unexpectedly from the suspension of judgment (epoche): having found equally balanced arguments on every question, the skeptic ceases to dogmatize and discovers that tranquility follows “as a shadow follows a body” (Sextus Empiricus, Outlines of Pyrrhonism I.29). The Stoics pursued a related but distinct state, apatheia—freedom from irrational passions (pathe)—achieved through the disciplined exercise of rational judgment and the recognition that only virtue is truly good.

Associated: Epicurus; Pyrrho; Sextus Empiricus, Outlines I; Stoics
D
Daimonion “Divine sign”

The mysterious inner voice or “divine something” (to daimonion) that Socrates reported experiencing throughout his life. It never told him what to do, only what not to do—it intervened as a prohibitive check, dissuading him from particular courses of action without providing positive guidance. In the Apology (31c–d), Socrates describes it as beginning in childhood and explains that it was the daimonion that prevented him from entering politics: had he done so, he says, he would long since have been killed and would have been of no use to Athens or to himself. The nature of the daimonion was one of the charges brought against him at trial (introducing “new divine things,” kaina daimonia). It has been variously interpreted as conscience, moral intuition, a genuine religious experience, a literary device of Plato’s, or (by modern commentators of a clinical bent) as a form of auditory hallucination—interpretations that reveal as much about the interpreters as about Socrates.

Associated: Plato, Apology, Phaedrus, Theaetetus; Xenophon, Memorabilia
Diairesis “Division”

The method of systematic division, paired with synagoge (collection), that Plato developed in his later dialogues as a refinement of the Socratic elenchus. The procedure is to begin with a broad genus, divide it into two species by identifying a relevant differentia, select the species that contains the target concept, and divide again, continuing until one arrives at the indivisible species that constitutes the sought-for definition. In the Sophist (218b–221c), the Eleatic Stranger famously demonstrates the method by first defining the angler (as a practitioner of the art of acquisition, by capture, by hunting, of water animals, by striking, with a hook, from below), then applies it to the far more philosophically significant task of defining the sophist. In the Phaedrus (265d–266c), Socrates describes the complementary operations: collection (synagoge) gathers scattered particulars under a single Form, while division carves the Form “at its natural joints, like a good butcher.” Aristotle adopted and transformed diairesis into his biological classification method, which organizes species by genus and differentia—the system that underwrites his zoological treatises and, ultimately, the entire Linnaean taxonomy.

Associated: Plato, Sophist, Statesman, Phaedrus; Aristotle, Parts of Animals I
Dikaiosyne “Justice,” “righteousness”

The central concept of Plato’s Republic and one of the most debated terms in ancient ethics. The dialogue begins with the question “What is justice?” and proceeds through increasingly sophisticated answers: justice as telling the truth and paying debts (Cephalus), as helping friends and harming enemies (Polemarchus), as the advantage of the stronger (Thrasymachus). Plato’s own answer, developed through the analogy between soul and city, defines justice as the harmonious ordering in which each part performs its proper function: in the soul, reason rules, spirit supports, and appetite obeys; in the city, the philosopher-rulers govern, the guardians defend, and the producers provide. Justice is thus not an external constraint but the internal health of the soul, valuable in itself regardless of its consequences. For Aristotle (Nicomachean Ethics V), dikaiosyne is the broadest of the virtues—“complete virtue in relation to others”—and encompasses both distributive justice (the fair allocation of goods according to merit) and corrective justice (the rectification of wrongs in voluntary and involuntary transactions).

Associated: Plato, Republic I–IV; Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics V
Doxa “Opinion,” “belief,” “appearance”

Opinion or belief, systematically distinguished from knowledge (episteme) throughout Greek philosophy. In Parmenides’ poem, the “Way of Opinion” (hodos doxes) is the unreliable path of mortal belief, contrasted with the “Way of Truth” (hodos aletheies). Plato develops this distinction architecturally in the Republic (509d–511e): the Divided Line assigns doxa to the lower portion, which grasps the sensible world of becoming (images and physical objects), while episteme occupies the upper portion, grasping the intelligible world of being (mathematical objects and the Forms). Doxa can be true or false, but even true opinion is inferior to knowledge because it lacks the rational account (logos) that secures it. In the Meno (97a–98b), Socrates illustrates this with the metaphor of the statues of Daedalus: true opinions are like untethered statues that wander away unless they are “tied down” by reasoning into knowledge. The Platonic depreciation of doxa stands in sharp contrast to the Sophistic and later Skeptical views, which treated opinion as the inescapable condition of human cognition.

Associated: Parmenides; Plato, Republic V–VII, Meno, Theaetetus
Dynamis “Potentiality,” “power,” “capacity”

One of Aristotle’s most important and original metaphysical concepts, paired with energeia (actuality). Dynamis is the capacity or potential for a thing to be or become something other than what it currently is: the acorn has the dynamis to become an oak, the block of marble the dynamis to become a statue, the student the dynamis to become a knower. Aristotle introduces the distinction in Metaphysics IX to solve the problem bequeathed by the Megarians, who argued that a thing can only do what it is actually doing (a builder can only build when actually building). Aristotle counters that dynamis is a real feature of the world: the builder retains the capacity to build even while sleeping. The relationship between dynamis and energeia is one of ontological priority: actuality is always prior to potentiality, both logically (you must know what an oak is to understand the acorn’s potential) and temporally (an actual oak always precedes the acorn). This principle culminates in the Unmoved Mover, which is pure energeia without any dynamis—pure actuality with no unrealized potential.

Associated: Aristotle, Metaphysics V, IX; Physics III
E
Eidos “Form,” “Idea,” “species”

The most important and most contested term in the Platonic-Aristotelian tradition. Etymologically related to the verb “to see” (idein), it originally meant “visible form” or “shape.” Plato transformed it into a technical metaphysical term: the eidos (or idea) is the eternal, unchanging, purely intelligible reality in which particular sensible things “participate” (methexis) or which they “imitate” (mimesis). The Form of Beauty is that by virtue of which all beautiful things are beautiful; the Form of Justice is that by which all just acts are just. The Forms exist independently of the sensible world and are known by the intellect alone, not by the senses. Aristotle retained the term but relocated the form: it is not a separate, transcendent entity but the intrinsic organizing principle of each substance—the form (eidos or morphe) that makes a thing what it is, inseparable from the matter (hyle) it informs except in thought. “The form is the what-it-was-to-be-that-thing” (to ti en einai), the essence grasped in definition. This disagreement between Plato and Aristotle about the status of form—transcendent versus immanent, separate versus inherent—is arguably the central philosophical debate of antiquity.

Associated: Plato, Phaedo, Republic, Parmenides; Aristotle, Metaphysics VII; Categories
Elenchus “Refutation,” “cross-examination”

The Socratic method of philosophical inquiry as practiced in Plato’s early dialogues. The procedure has a consistent structure: (1) the interlocutor proposes a definition of a virtue (e.g., “courage is standing firm in battle”); (2) Socrates elicits from the interlocutor additional beliefs that seem obviously true; (3) Socrates demonstrates that these additional beliefs logically contradict the original definition; (4) the interlocutor is forced to retract or modify his claim; (5) the cycle repeats with revised definitions, typically ending in aporia. The philosophical significance of the elenchus is debated: does it merely expose inconsistency in the interlocutor’s beliefs (a negative, destructive function), or does it somehow point toward the truth (a positive, constructive function)? Gregory Vlastos argued influentially that Socrates treats the elenchus as a test of truth, not merely of consistency—that the beliefs that survive elenctic testing are, for Socrates, thereby established as true. This interpretation remains controversial but has defined the terms of scholarly debate. The elenchus is the ancestor of all dialectical methods in Western philosophy, from Aristotle’s dialectic through Hegel’s to the Socratic method still used in legal education today.

Associated: Plato, Euthyphro, Laches, Charmides, Protagoras
Energeia “Actuality,” “activity,” “being-at-work”

Aristotle’s term for the fully realized state of a thing’s nature, the complement of dynamis (potentiality). The word is Aristotle’s own coinage, derived from ergon (work, function): energeia is literally “being-at-work,” the active exercise of a capacity. Seeing is the energeia of the eye; contemplation is the energeia of the intellect; living well is the energeia of the human being as a whole. Aristotle also uses the closely related term entelecheia (“having-its-end-within”), sometimes interchangeably, sometimes with subtle distinctions that scholars debate. The Unmoved Mover is pure energeia—“thought thinking itself” (noesis noeseos noesis)—with no unrealized potentiality. The concept transforms the entire structure of Aristotle’s metaphysics, biology, and ethics: the soul is the energeia of the body; eudaimonia is the energeia of the soul in accordance with virtue; the cosmos itself is kept in motion by its desire to imitate the pure energeia of the divine.

Associated: Aristotle, Metaphysics IX, XII; De Anima II; Nicomachean Ethics I, X
Epoche “Suspension of judgment”

The deliberate withholding of assent to any non-evident proposition, the defining practice of both Academic and Pyrrhonian skepticism. Arcesilaus introduced epoche into the Academy as the proper response to the Stoic claim that “cataleptic impressions” provide an infallible criterion of truth: since no impression can be shown to be certainly true, the wise person suspends judgment on all matters. The Pyrrhonists made epoche the cornerstone of their entire way of life: Sextus Empiricus describes the skeptic as one who, having investigated a question and found equally balanced arguments on both sides (isostheneia), suspends judgment and discovers that tranquility (ataraxia) follows spontaneously. Crucially, epoche applies only to theoretical propositions about the hidden nature of things, not to the appearances themselves: the skeptic freely reports how things appear (the honey appears sweet) but refuses to affirm how things are in their nature (the honey is sweet). Husserl later adopted the term epoche for his phenomenological “bracketing” of the natural attitude, a debt he explicitly acknowledged.

Associated: Arcesilaus; Sextus Empiricus, Outlines of Pyrrhonism I; Husserl
Episteme “Knowledge,” “science,” “understanding”

Genuine, secure, rationally grounded knowledge, systematically distinguished from doxa (opinion). For Plato, episteme is the soul’s apprehension of the eternal, unchanging Forms; it is infallible, always true, and accompanied by a rational account (logos) that can explain why the thing known is as it is. The Theaetetus examines three proposed definitions of knowledge—perception, true judgment, and true judgment with an account—and finds all three wanting, leaving the question unresolved (aporetic). For Aristotle, episteme in the strict sense is demonstrative knowledge: to know something scientifically is to know it through its causes, to know that it cannot be otherwise, and to derive it by valid syllogistic reasoning from necessarily true first principles (Posterior Analytics 71b9–72a5). This conception of knowledge as demonstrative, causal, and necessary dominated Western epistemology through the early modern period and was the standard against which Descartes, Hume, and Kant measured their own theories of knowledge.

Associated: Plato, Theaetetus, Republic V–VII; Aristotle, Posterior Analytics I
Eros “Love,” “desire,” “longing”

In Plato’s Symposium, eros is philosophically transformed from sexual desire into the soul’s fundamental drive toward beauty, truth, and the eternal. In Diotima’s speech (201d–212b), Socrates recounts the teachings of a wise woman who described eros as a daimon—an intermediary between mortal and divine—born of Poverty (Penia) and Resource (Poros). Eros is neither beautiful nor ugly, neither wise nor ignorant, but always striving, always “in between.” The “Ladder of Love” (scala amoris) describes the soul’s ascent: from love of one beautiful body, to love of all beautiful bodies, to love of beautiful souls, to love of beautiful practices and laws, to love of beautiful knowledge, and finally to the sudden vision of Beauty itself (auto to kalon)—eternal, absolute, unmixed, the Form of Beauty. In the Phaedrus (244a–257b), eros is a form of divine madness that recollects the soul’s pre-natal vision of the Forms: the lover sees in the beloved a reflection of the eternal Beauty and is drawn upward through philosophical conversation toward the Forms themselves. Ficino’s fifteenth-century commentary on the Symposium coined the term amor platonicus, “Platonic love,” which entered European literature and has shaped Western conceptions of love ever since.

Associated: Plato, Symposium, Phaedrus; Ficino, De Amore
Eudaimonia “Happiness,” “flourishing,” “well-being”

The central concept of Greek ethical theory, literally “having a good daimon” (guiding spirit). All ancient schools agreed that eudaimonia is the highest good and the ultimate goal of human life; they disagreed profoundly about what it consists in. For Aristotle, eudaimonia is “activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, and if there are several virtues, in accordance with the best and most complete, in a complete life” (Nicomachean Ethics 1098a16–18). It is not a feeling or a subjective state but an objective condition of living well and doing well (eu zen kai eu prattein). It requires not only virtue but also external goods (health, wealth, friends, good fortune)—a position that distinguishes Aristotle from the Stoics, who held that virtue is sufficient for eudaimonia regardless of external circumstances. For Epicurus, eudaimonia consists in pleasure (understood as the absence of pain and disturbance). For the Skeptics, it is ataraxia (tranquility) achieved through suspension of judgment. The contemporary revival of virtue ethics, led by Elizabeth Anscombe, Philippa Foot, and Alasdair MacIntyre, has returned eudaimonia to the center of moral philosophy, arguing that modern ethical theories (Kantian and utilitarian) lost something essential by abandoning the ancient framework of human flourishing.

Associated: Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics I, X; Plato, Republic; Epicurus; the Stoics
H
Hexis “Disposition,” “stable state,” “habit”

A settled condition or stable disposition of the soul, acquired through repeated practice rather than innate endowment. In Aristotle’s ethics, moral virtue (arete) is defined as a hexis prohairetike—a “dispositional state concerned with choice”—that aims at the mean between excess and deficiency (Nicomachean Ethics 1106b36). Just as a person becomes a builder by building and a harpist by playing the harp, one becomes courageous by performing courageous acts and temperate by practicing temperance. The emphasis on hexis makes Aristotle’s ethics fundamentally a theory of character formation through habituation (ethismos): moral education is not primarily about learning rules or principles but about developing stable dispositions to feel the right emotions, at the right time, toward the right objects, in the right way. This insight has been central to the contemporary revival of virtue ethics and to its dialogue with moral psychology and cognitive science.

Associated: Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics II; Categories 8
Hyle “Matter,” “material”

Originally “wood” or “timber,” Aristotle adopted the term as a technical designation for the material substratum that, together with form (eidos, morphe), constitutes every natural substance. The bronze is the hyle of the statue; the letters are the hyle of the syllable; the bodily organs are the hyle of the living animal. Hyle is that which is potentially something but actually nothing determinate until informed by form: “prime matter” (prote hyle), the ultimate material substratum, is pure potentiality without any actual characteristics of its own—a concept that generated enormous debate among Aristotle’s ancient and medieval commentators (is prime matter a genuine entity or merely an abstraction?). The form-matter composite (synolon) is the primary substance in the physical world: neither form nor matter exists independently in nature; they are always found together, distinguishable in thought but inseparable in reality. This “hylomorphic” analysis (a term coined by later scholars from hyle + morphe) became the dominant framework of natural philosophy from Aristotle through the scholastic period.

Associated: Aristotle, Physics I–II; Metaphysics VII–IX
K
Katharsis “Purification,” “purgation,” “clarification”

One of the most famous and most disputed terms in all of Aristotle. In the Poetics (1449b24–28), Aristotle defines tragedy as “the imitation of an action that is serious, complete, and of a certain magnitude,” which “through pity and fear achieves the katharsis of such emotions.” The precise meaning of katharsis here has been debated for centuries: does it mean the purgation of pity and fear (a medical metaphor: tragedy drains off an excess of dangerous emotion, providing therapeutic relief)? Or the purification of pity and fear (a religious metaphor: tragedy refines and educates the emotions, teaching the audience to feel appropriately)? Or the intellectual clarification of the events that arouse pity and fear (a cognitive interpretation: tragedy helps the audience understand the human condition)? The debate is complicated by the fact that Aristotle promised a fuller discussion of katharsis in a lost second book of the Poetics (possibly treating comedy), which does not survive. The term also appears in Plato’s Phaedo (67c) in a philosophical-religious sense: philosophy is the “purification” of the soul from the body and its desires, preparing the soul for the vision of truth after death.

Associated: Aristotle, Poetics; Plato, Phaedo; Politics VIII
Kosmos “Order,” “world-order,” “universe”

Originally meaning “order,” “arrangement,” or “adornment” (English “cosmetic” derives from the same root), the term was applied to the universe as a whole by the Pythagoreans, who held that the world-order is a harmonious, mathematically structured whole—a kosmos rather than a chaos. Heraclitus used the term in his famous fragment: “This kosmos, the same for all, no god or man made, but it always was and is and shall be: an ever-living fire, kindling in measures and going out in measures” (DK 22 B30). For Plato, the kosmos is the product of a divine craftsman (demiurgos) who shapes pre-existing matter according to the model of the eternal Forms, producing “a living creature endowed with soul and reason” (Timaeus 30b)—the universe as a single, ensouled, rational organism. For Aristotle, the kosmos is eternal and uncreated, a finite sphere with the earth at its center, organized into concentric celestial spheres whose circular motions are sustained by their desire to imitate the perfection of the Unmoved Mover. The Stoics identified the kosmos with God: the universe is a living, rational, providential whole, permeated by the divine Logos. The Epicureans alone rejected cosmic order: their universe is infinite, purposeless, and composed of atoms falling through void.

Associated: Pythagoras; Heraclitus; Plato, Timaeus; Aristotle, De Caelo; Stoics; Epicurus
L
Logos “Reason,” “word,” “account,” “argument”

Perhaps the richest and most untranslatable term in the Greek philosophical vocabulary. Its semantic range includes: word, speech, statement, argument, reason, rational principle, proportion, definition, account, explanation, and discourse. In Heraclitus, the Logos is the rational principle governing the cosmos, the hidden structure of reality that most people fail to grasp: “Of this Logos which is always, men prove to be uncomprehending, both before they hear and after they have heard it” (DK 22 B1). In Plato, logos appears in multiple senses: as reasoned argument (the medium of dialectic), as rational account (the logos that distinguishes knowledge from true opinion in the Theaetetus and Meno), and as the rational part of the soul (to logistikon) that should rule the other parts. In Aristotle, logos is the defining capacity of the human being: the zoon logon echon, the “living being having logos”—commonly translated as “rational animal.” In the Politics (1253a), Aristotle distinguishes human logos from animal phone (voice): while animals can signal pleasure and pain, only logos enables discourse about justice and injustice, good and evil. The Stoics identified the Logos with God, Nature, Fate, and Providence—the rational, fiery breath (pneuma) that pervades and governs all things. In early Christianity, the Gospel of John opens: “In the beginning was the Logos, and the Logos was with God, and the Logos was God”—a passage whose philosophical resonance with the Heraclitean and Stoic traditions was recognized by the earliest Church Fathers.

Associated: Heraclitus; Plato, Theaetetus, Republic; Aristotle, Politics I; Stoics; Gospel of John
M
Methexis “Participation,” “partaking”

The relationship by which sensible particulars are connected to the Platonic Forms. A beautiful thing is beautiful by “participating in” or “partaking of” the Form of Beauty; a just act is just by participating in the Form of Justice. The precise nature of this relationship is the most persistent problem in Plato’s metaphysics. In the Phaedo (100c–101c), Socrates states that things are beautiful “by the Beautiful” but confesses uncertainty about whether this means by participation, by presence, or by communion. In the Parmenides (130a–135c), the young Socrates faces devastating criticism from the elder Parmenides: if the Form is wholly present in each particular, it is divided against itself; if only a part is present, the Form is fragmented. The “Third Man Argument” (132a–133a) shows that explaining the resemblance between particulars and their Form requires postulating a second Form, which in turn requires a third, generating an infinite regress. Whether Plato considered these objections fatal or resolvable remains one of the central interpretive questions of Platonic scholarship. Aristotle took them as decisive refutations, arguing that form must be immanent in substance rather than separate from it.

Associated: Plato, Phaedo, Parmenides, Republic; Aristotle, Metaphysics I, VII
Mimesis “Imitation,” “representation”

The concept of imitation or representation, central to both metaphysics and aesthetics. In Plato’s Republic (X, 595a–608b), mimesis is deployed as a critique of art: the painter who depicts a bed produces an image of an image—a copy of the particular bed, which is itself a copy of the Form of Bed. Art is thus “at three removes from the truth” and, by appealing to the irrational, emotional part of the soul, potentially dangerous to the moral health of the city. This leads to the notorious proposal to banish most poets from the ideal state. Aristotle’s Poetics responds with a radically different valuation: mimesis is not a deficiency but a fundamental human capacity. “The instinct of imitation is implanted in man from childhood; he differs from other animals in being the most imitative” (Poetics 1448b5). Poetry is “more philosophical and more serious than history,” because history tells what happened while poetry tells what might happen—it deals with universals, not particulars (1451b5–7). The transformation of mimesis from Platonic suspicion to Aristotelian celebration is one of the pivotal moments in the history of Western aesthetics.

Associated: Plato, Republic III, X; Aristotle, Poetics
N
Nous “Mind,” “intellect,” “reason”

The highest cognitive faculty, the capacity for intellectual intuition of first principles. In Anaxagoras, Nous is the cosmic Mind that initiated the rotation of the primordial mixture and ordered the cosmos—the first explicit principle of intelligence in Greek philosophy. In Plato, nous is the soul’s capacity for direct apprehension of the Forms, the highest segment of the Divided Line (Republic 511d–e), where the soul grasps the first principles of reality without reliance on sensory images or hypotheses. In Aristotle, nous is the faculty by which we grasp the indemonstrable first principles of scientific knowledge (Posterior Analytics II.19)—the axioms and definitions that cannot themselves be demonstrated but are the starting points from which all demonstration proceeds. In the De Anima (III.5), Aristotle introduces the famous and deeply obscure distinction between passive intellect (nous pathetikos), which receives intelligible forms, and agent intellect (nous poietikos), which “makes all things” as light makes potential colors actual. The agent intellect is described as separate, impassible, and immortal. The interpretation of this passage generated over two millennia of debate: Alexander of Aphrodisias identified the agent intellect with God; Averroes argued for a single shared intellect for all humanity; Aquinas maintained that each individual possesses a personal agent intellect. In Plotinus, Nous is the second hypostasis, the divine Intellect that emanates from the One and contains within itself the totality of the Platonic Forms.

Associated: Anaxagoras; Plato, Republic VI–VII; Aristotle, De Anima III, Posterior Analytics II.19; Plotinus, Enneads V
O
Ousia “Substance,” “being,” “essence”

The central term of Aristotle’s metaphysics and arguably the most important concept in the history of Western ontology. Derived from the participle of the verb “to be” (einai), ousia literally means “being-ness.” Plato uses it to refer to the true being of the Forms, the “really real” as opposed to the shadowy becoming of the sensible world. Aristotle transforms it into the concept of substance: the primary existent, the individual thing that is the subject of all predication but is itself predicated of nothing. In the Categories (2a11–14), primary ousia is the individual (this particular man, this particular horse); secondary ousia is the species and genus (man, animal). In the Metaphysics (VII–IX), the question “What is ousia?” becomes “the question that was asked of old, is asked now, and will always be asked” (1028b2–4), and Aristotle explores multiple candidates: the substratum (hypokeimenon), the essence (to ti en einai), the universal, and the genus, ultimately arguing that form (eidos) is substance in the primary sense. The Nicene Creed’s declaration that the Son is homoousios (“of one substance”) with the Father shows how deeply ousia penetrated Christian theology.

Associated: Aristotle, Categories, Metaphysics VII–IX; Plato; Nicene Creed
P
Paideia “Education,” “culture,” “formation”

The comprehensive process of cultural and intellectual formation through which a human being achieves full humanity. More than mere instruction or schooling, paideia encompasses the total development of mind, character, and civic capacity. Werner Jaeger’s monumental study Paideia: The Ideals of Greek Culture (3 vols., 1933–1947) traced the evolution of the concept from the Homeric aristocratic ideal of the warrior-hero through the Sophistic teaching of rhetorical and political competence to the Platonic and Aristotelian vision of philosophical education as the formation of the soul. In Plato’s Republic, the education of the philosopher-rulers is the centerpiece of the political program: beginning with gymnastics and music in childhood, progressing through mathematics and dialectic in young adulthood, and culminating in the vision of the Form of the Good after years of philosophical training. The Allegory of the Cave is itself a parable of paideia: the prisoner’s painful ascent from darkness to light is the soul’s journey from ignorance to knowledge. For Aristotle, paideia is inseparable from the life of the polis: “It is clear that education must be one and the same for all, and its provision a matter of public, not private, action” (Politics 1337a).

Associated: Plato, Republic II–VII; Aristotle, Politics VIII; Jaeger, Paideia
Phronesis “Practical wisdom,” “prudence”

The intellectual virtue that governs practical reasoning and moral judgment. In Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics (VI.5, 1140a24–1140b30), phronesis is distinguished from theoretical wisdom (sophia), which contemplates eternal truths, and from techne (craft knowledge), which produces external products. Phronesis is the ability to deliberate well about what is good for oneself and for human beings in general—not in the abstract, but in the concrete circumstances of action, where universal principles must be applied to particular situations. The phronimos (the practically wise person) perceives the morally salient features of a situation, weighs competing considerations, and acts rightly. Aristotle’s paradigmatic example is Pericles: a man of practical judgment who could discern what is good in particular circumstances. Phronesis cannot be reduced to a set of rules or a decision procedure; it requires experience, emotional maturity, and a kind of moral perception that develops only through years of practice. This concept has been foundational for the contemporary revival of virtue ethics, particularly in the work of Martha Nussbaum and John McDowell, and for Hans-Georg Gadamer’s philosophical hermeneutics, which takes phronesis as the model for all interpretive understanding.

Associated: Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics VI; Gadamer, Truth and Method
Physis “Nature,” “growth,” “the natural”

The nature or essence of a thing; more broadly, the natural world as a whole, as opposed to convention, custom, or human artifice (nomos, techne). The Pre-Socratic inquiry was called peri physeos historia (“investigation concerning nature”), and many early philosophical works bore the title Peri Physeos (On Nature). For Aristotle, physis is the internal principle of motion and rest that belongs to natural beings as such (Physics 192b21–23): a stone falls downward and fire rises upward by physis; a bed does not sprout because its motion is imposed by techne, not by its own nature. Aristotle distinguishes between physis as matter (the material from which a thing is made) and physis as form (the structure or essence that makes it what it is), arguing that form is physis in the primary sense. The physis/nomos distinction was a central topic of Sophistic debate: is morality natural or conventional? Are social hierarchies grounded in nature or imposed by custom? Hippias and Antiphon argued for the primacy of physis; Protagoras and the democratic tradition emphasized nomos. The Stoics later unified the two: the moral law is the law of nature, and living virtuously is living in accordance with physis.

Associated: Pre-Socratics; Aristotle, Physics II; the Sophists; Stoics
Polis “City,” “city-state,” “political community”

The self-governing city-state, the fundamental unit of Greek political life and the natural horizon of human flourishing in both Plato’s and Aristotle’s political thought. In the Republic, Plato constructs the ideal polis as an analogy for the soul: its three classes (rulers, guardians, producers) correspond to the three parts of the soul (reason, spirit, appetite), and justice in the city consists in each class performing its proper function. In the Politics (1253a1–4), Aristotle makes the celebrated claim that “man is by nature a political animal” (zoon politikon)—human beings are naturally constituted for life in a political community, and anyone who lives outside the polis by nature (not by misfortune) is either a beast or a god. The polis exists not merely for the sake of life (security and economic exchange) but for the sake of the good life (eu zen)—the life of virtue, friendship, and intellectual activity that constitutes eudaimonia. The Greek polis was small enough (typically 1,000 to 50,000 citizens) that direct participation in governance was possible, giving the concept of citizenship a concreteness and intensity that modern representative democracy lacks. Alexander’s conquests effectively ended the era of the independent polis, inaugurating the Hellenistic period in which philosophy shifted from political engagement to individual ethics—a shift reflected in the Stoic, Epicurean, and Skeptical movements.

Associated: Plato, Republic, Laws; Aristotle, Politics I, III
Psyche “Soul,” “mind,” “life-principle”

The animating principle of living things, one of the most important and most variously interpreted concepts in ancient philosophy. In Homer, psyche is the shade that departs the body at death and persists as a feeble, witless ghost in Hades. The Pythagoreans and Orphics transformed it into an immortal, transmigrating entity whose true home is the divine realm and whose incarnation in a body is a punishment or trial. Plato synthesized and elevated these traditions: in the Phaedo, the psyche is immortal, rational, and kin to the Forms; in the Republic (435a–441c), it is divided into three parts—rational (logistikon), spirited (thumoeides), and appetitive (epithumetikon)—whose harmonious ordering constitutes justice in the individual. In the Phaedrus (246a–249d), the soul is likened to a winged charioteer driving two horses, one noble and one unruly, toward the vision of the Forms. Aristotle radically reconceived the psyche: it is not a separate substance imprisoned in a body but the form (eidos) of a natural body that has life potentially—“the first actuality of a natural body that has organs” (De Anima 412a27–28). The soul is to the body as sight is to the eye: the functional organization that makes the living organism what it is. This definition applies to all living things: plants have a nutritive soul, animals a sensitive soul, humans a rational soul. Whether the rational soul (or some part of it) survives the death of the body remains one of the most debated questions in Aristotelian scholarship.

Associated: Plato, Phaedo, Republic IV, Phaedrus; Aristotle, De Anima II–III
S
Sophia “Wisdom”

The highest form of knowledge, directed toward the most fundamental and universal truths. In ordinary Greek usage, sophia could denote any form of expertise or skill (a carpenter or navigator could be called sophos). The philosophers claimed the term for knowledge of the highest and most universal kind: the principles and causes of all things. The word “philosophy” (philosophia) means literally “love of sophia”—a term tradition attributes to Pythagoras, who reportedly said that no human being possesses sophia (which belongs to the gods alone), but can only aspire to it as a lover aspires to the beloved. Aristotle distinguishes sophia from phronesis (practical wisdom) in Nicomachean Ethics VI: sophia is the combination of nous (intuitive grasp of first principles) and episteme (demonstrative knowledge derived from them), directed toward eternal, necessary truths. It is the highest virtue of the theoretical intellect and the activity in which human eudaimonia is most fully realized (Nicomachean Ethics X.7–8).

Associated: Pythagoras; Plato, Republic VI–VII; Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics VI, X; Metaphysics I
Sophrosyne “Temperance,” “moderation,” “self-knowledge”

One of the four cardinal virtues in the Greek ethical tradition, associated with the Delphic maxim “Know thyself” (gnothi seauton). Its semantic field is extraordinarily rich: it encompasses self-control, moderation, soundness of mind, sanity, chastity, and the quiet inner order of a person who is master of his or her desires. In Plato’s Charmides, the dialogue attempts (inconclusively) to define sophrosyne, considering proposals including “doing one’s own business quietly,” “a sense of shame,” and “self-knowledge.” In the Republic (430d–432a), sophrosyne is the virtue of the appetitive part of the soul and the productive class of the city: it consists in the harmonious agreement of all parts about which should rule (reason) and which should be ruled (appetite). For Aristotle, sophrosyne is the virtue concerned specifically with bodily pleasures of touch and taste (Nicomachean Ethics III.10–12)—a significantly narrower scope than Plato’s use. Helen North’s landmark study Sophrosyne: Self-Knowledge and Self-Restraint in Greek Literature traces the concept’s evolution from Homer through the classical period, demonstrating its centrality to Greek ethical and political thought.

Associated: Plato, Charmides, Republic IV; Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics III
T
Techne “Art,” “craft,” “skill”

A systematic, rational capacity for making or producing, distinguished from both theoretical knowledge (episteme) and practical wisdom (phronesis). The Socratic dialogues frequently explore the analogy between techne and virtue: just as the cobbler knows how to make shoes and the navigator knows how to steer a ship, the virtuous person should know how to live well. The analogy creates a persistent difficulty: if virtue is a techne, it should be teachable (as the Sophists claimed); but Socrates consistently fails to identify either the product it creates or the expert teachers who transmit it. For Aristotle, techne is a form of knowledge (Nicomachean Ethics VI.4, 1140a1–23): the person with techne knows not only how to produce something but why—he can give a rational account (logos) of the process. This distinguishes techne from mere experience (empeiria): the experienced doctor knows that a certain remedy works; the doctor with techne knows why it works. Techne belongs to the sphere of the contingent (what can be otherwise), unlike episteme, which deals with the necessary. From techne derives “technology,” though the ancient concept encompasses far more than machinery: it includes medicine, rhetoric, music, architecture, and all forms of skilled production.

Associated: Plato, Gorgias, Ion; Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics VI; Metaphysics I
Telos “End,” “goal,” “purpose”

The end, completion, or purpose toward which a process is directed. For Aristotle, telos is the most important of the four causes: it is “that for the sake of which” a thing exists or a process occurs. The telos of the acorn is the oak tree; the telos of the eye is seeing; the telos of the human being is eudaimonia. Aristotle’s natural philosophy is pervasively teleological: nature acts for an end, and the end is the form in its fully realized state. “Nature does nothing in vain” is a principle he invokes repeatedly in his biological works. This teleological worldview was the most distinctive and the most contested feature of Aristotelian natural philosophy. The mechanical philosophy of the seventeenth century (Descartes, Hobbes, Boyle) explicitly rejected teleological explanation in physics, and Darwin’s theory of natural selection provided a non-teleological account of the apparent purposiveness of biological structures. Whether Aristotle’s teleology is genuinely committed to cosmic purpose or is merely a heuristic for understanding functional organization in living systems remains a topic of active scholarly debate. In ethics, telos is the concept that organizes all value: the good for each thing is its telos, and the supreme good for human beings is the telos of human life as a whole.

Associated: Aristotle, Physics II; Metaphysics V; Nicomachean Ethics I; Parts of Animals I
Theoria “Contemplation,” “theoretical inquiry”

The activity of the intellect directed toward understanding for its own sake, without practical or productive aim. Etymologically related to thea (“a viewing, a spectacle”) and theoros (“a spectator”), theoria carries the connotation of pure, disinterested seeing—the mind’s contemplation of reality as it is. Aristotle assigns theoria the highest place in his hierarchy of human activities: theoretical knowledge (episteme theoretike) is superior to practical and productive knowledge because it is pursued for its own sake, deals with eternal and necessary truths, and exercises the highest human faculty (nous). In Nicomachean Ethics X.7–8 (1177a12–1178a8), Aristotle argues that the contemplative life (bios theoretikos) is the most complete eudaimonia: it is the most continuous, most self-sufficient, most pleasant, and most divine of all human activities, for “the activity of God, which surpasses all others in blessedness, must be contemplative.” The tension between the contemplative life and the active, political life (bios politikos)—a tension already present in Plato’s Cave Allegory, where the philosopher who has seen the light is compelled to return to the cave and govern—has been one of the enduring questions of Western ethical and political thought.

Associated: Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics X; Metaphysics I, XII; Plato, Republic VII
To Kalon “The beautiful,” “the noble,” “the fine”

A concept that unites aesthetic and moral value in a way that has no exact English equivalent. Kalon means simultaneously “beautiful” (in the aesthetic sense: a beautiful painting, a beautiful body) and “noble” or “fine” (in the moral sense: a noble deed, a fine character). This fusion of beauty and goodness is fundamental to Greek ethical thought: the virtuous act is not merely right but kalon—beautiful, admirable, worthy of praise. In Plato’s Symposium (210a–212a), the ascent of eros leads from beautiful bodies to beautiful souls to beautiful practices to “the Beautiful itself” (auto to kalon)—the Form of Beauty, eternal, absolute, and unmixed with anything corporeal. In Aristotle, to kalon is the proper object of virtuous action: the courageous person acts for the sake of to kalon (Nicomachean Ethics 1115b12), choosing the noble deed because it is noble, not because it is advantageous. The concept implies that moral goodness has an intrinsic radiance or attractiveness—that virtue is not merely dutiful but genuinely beautiful.

Associated: Plato, Symposium, Hippias Major; Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics III–IV; Poetics
