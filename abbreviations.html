Citation Reference

Abbreviations

The standard editions, translations, and classical numbering systems used throughout the archive’s summaries, quotations, and commentary.

Citations in this archive follow the conventions of academic philosophy: classical authors (Plato, Aristotle) are cited by the traditional numerical systems that remain stable across all translations; modern authors are cited by the standard critical editions in the original language, with the translation abbreviation where applicable.

Where a citation appears as, e.g., AT VII, 25; CSM II, 17— this means: Adam-Tannery edition, volume VII, page 25; Cottingham-Stoothoff-Murdoch English translation, volume II, page 17.

I · Classical Numbering Systems
Plato 428–348 BC
Stephanus Platonis Opera, ed. H. Estienne (Stephanus) The standard page-and-section references for Plato, dating from Henri Estienne’s 1578 edition. Every Plato translation in any language prints Stephanus numbers in the margins. The number refers to the page and section letter (a–e) of that edition. Example: Republic 505a — Meno 80d — Phaedo 118a
Apo. Apology Standard dialogue abbreviations are used in scholarly citations alongside Stephanus numbers. Other common abbreviations: Rep. (Republic), Phd. (Phaedo), Sym. (Symposium), Tim. (Timaeus), Men. (Meno), Phdr. (Phaedrus), Tht. (Theaetetus), Parm. (Parmenides), Soph. (Sophist), Pol. (Statesman), Laws
Aristotle 384–322 BC
Bekker Aristotelis Opera, ed. I. Bekker (Berlin, 1831) Immanuel Bekker’s 1831 Berlin edition established the standard page-line references for Aristotle. Every modern translation prints Bekker numbers (page, column a or b, line) in the margins. Example: NE 1098a16 — Phys. 192b21 — Met. 1028b2
NE Nicomachean Ethics Standard Aristotelian treatise abbreviations. Other common: Met. (Metaphysics), Phys. (Physics), DA (De Anima / On the Soul), Pol. (Politics), Poet. (Poetics), Rhet. (Rhetoric), Cat. (Categories), APo. (Posterior Analytics), APr. (Prior Analytics), DC (De Caelo), GA (Generation of Animals), PA (Parts of Animals), HA (History of Animals), EE (Eudemian Ethics), Protr. (Protrepticus)
II · Pre-Socratics & Hellenistic Sources
Pre-Socratics c. 624–400 BC
DK Diels-Kranz, Die Fragmente der Vorsokratiker Hermann Diels’s 1903 collection of Pre-Socratic fragments and testimonies, revised by Walther Kranz (6th ed., 1951). The standard reference for all Pre-Socratic philosophers. Each philosopher is assigned a number; “A” fragments are testimonia (reports about the philosopher by others), “B” fragments are direct quotations. Example: DK 22 B30 = Heraclitus (no. 22), direct fragment no. 30 — DK 28 B1 = Parmenides, direct fragment no. 1 — DK 12 B1 = Anaximander, fragment no. 1
DL Diogenes Laërtius, Lives of the Eminent Philosophers The primary ancient biographical source for Greek philosophers, written c. 3rd century AD. Cited by book and section number. Example: DL 9.8 = Book IX, section 8 (on Heraclitus)
SVF Von Arnim, Stoicorum Veterum Fragmenta (1903–05) The standard collection of early Stoic fragments (Zeno, Cleanthes, Chrysippus and their school), assembled by Hans von Arnim. Four volumes; cited by fragment number. Example: SVF I, 45 = Zeno fragment 45 — SVF II, 836 = Chrysippus fragment 836
LS Long & Sedley, The Hellenistic Philosophers (1987) A. A. Long and D. N. Sedley’s authoritative two-volume sourcebook for Stoic, Epicurean, and Sceptical philosophy. Vol. I contains translations; vol. II the original texts. Cited by section letter/number. Example: LS 28A = section 28, passage A (on Stoic theology)
PH Sextus Empiricus, Outlines of Pyrrhonism (Pyrrhoniae Hypotyposes) The most complete surviving account of Pyrrhonian scepticism. Cited by book and section number. Example: PH I.29 = Book I, section 29
M Sextus Empiricus, Against the Mathematicians (Adversus Mathematicos) Sextus’s critique of every branch of dogmatic philosophy. Cited by book and section. Sometimes abbreviated AM. Example: M VII.65 = Book VII (Against the Logicians), section 65
III · Modern Philosopher Standard Editions
Descartes 1596–1650
AT Adam & Tannery, Œuvres de Descartes (Paris, 1897–1913; rev. 1964–76) The standard critical edition of Descartes’s complete works in French and Latin, 12 volumes. The reference used by all Descartes scholars worldwide. Cited as “AT” followed by the volume number (Roman) and page number (Arabic). Example: AT VII, 25 = volume VII, page 25 (Second Meditation) — AT VI, 32 = volume VI, page 32 (Discourse Part IV)
CSM Cottingham, Stoothoff & Murdoch, The Philosophical Writings of Descartes (Cambridge, 1984–85) The standard English translation of Descartes, 2 volumes (a third volume covering the correspondence, with A. Kenny, appeared in 1991). When a citation gives both AT and CSM references, AT is the original-language source and CSM is the English translation location. Example: AT VII, 25; CSM II, 17 — i.e. the passage is in Adam-Tannery vol. VII p. 25, translated in CSM vol. II p. 17
CSMK Cottingham, Stoothoff, Murdoch & Kenny, vol. III (Correspondence) Used specifically for citations from Descartes’s letters. Example: CSMK III, 147 = Cottingham et al., vol. III, p. 147
Spinoza 1632–1677
E Ethica Ordine Geometrico Demonstrata (Ethics) Cited by part (I–V), then by the element type and number. The geometric structure means citations are highly precise and translation-independent. Abbreviations: D = Definition, A = Axiom, P = Proposition, Dem. = Demonstration, C = Corollary, S = Scholium, Pref. = Preface, App. = Appendix
E I P11 Ethics, Part I, Proposition 11 Full citation format: Part number (Roman) + element type + element number. Example: E II P13S = Ethics II, Proposition 13, Scholium (on the body) — E IV Pref. = Ethics IV, Preface (“Deus sive Natura”)
TTP Tractatus Theologico-Politicus (1670) Cited by chapter and paragraph, or by page in the Gebhardt critical edition (Spinoza Opera, Heidelberg, 1925), abbreviated G. Example: TTP ch. 7 — G III, 98
Leibniz 1646–1716
GP Gerhardt, Die philosophischen Schriften von G. W. Leibniz (Berlin, 1875–90) The standard edition of Leibniz’s philosophical writings, 7 volumes. Cited by volume (Roman) and page (Arabic). Example: GP II, 269 = volume II, page 269 (letter to De Volder)
A Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz: Sämtliche Schriften und Briefe (Akademie-Ausgabe) The ongoing critical edition of all Leibniz’s writings, published by the Berlin-Brandenburg and Göttingen Academies of Sciences. Cited by series (Roman), volume (Roman), and page. Example: A VI, iv, 1529 = Series VI (philosophical writings), vol. iv, p. 1529
Mon. Monadologie (Monadology, 1714) Cited by section number (§). Other short Leibniz texts similarly cited. Example: Mon. §7 — DM §8 (Discourse on Metaphysics, section 8) — PNG §3 (Principles of Nature and Grace)
Locke 1632–1704
Essay An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1689) Cited by book (Roman numeral), chapter, and section. The standard critical edition is edited by P. H. Nidditch (Oxford, 1975). Example: Essay II.i.2 = Book II, chapter i, section 2 (on the mind as white paper)
TT Two Treatises of Government (1689) Cited by treatise number (I or II) and section. The standard edition is by P. Laslett (Cambridge, 1960). Example: TT II.6 = Second Treatise, section 6 (on the law of nature)
Hume 1711–1776
T A Treatise of Human Nature (1739–40) Cited by book, part, and section (e.g., T 1.4.6) or by page in the Selby-Bigge/Nidditch edition (Oxford, 1978), abbreviated SBN. Example: T 1.4.6 / SBN 252 = Book I, Part 4, Section 6 (bundle theory of self), Selby-Bigge p. 252
EHU An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding (1748) Cited by section and part, or by Selby-Bigge/Nidditch page number. Sometimes abbreviated simply Enquiry. Example: EHU 4.2 = Section IV, Part 2 (on the problem of induction)
DNR Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion (1779) Cited by part number. Published posthumously. Example: DNR IX = Part IX (Cleanthes on the cosmological argument)
Kant 1724–1804
A / B Critique of Pure Reason, first (A) and second (B) editions The Critique of Pure Reason appeared in two editions (1781, 1787) with significant revisions. Passages are cited by the pagination of the original editions, printed in the margins of all translations. “A” = first edition; “B” = second edition; passages in both editions give both references. Example: A444/B472 = the Third Antinomy, found at p. 444 in A edition and p. 472 in B edition — Bxxx = B edition, preface page xxx
KrV Kritik der reinen Vernunft (Critique of Pure Reason) The German abbreviation, used in German-language scholarship and occasionally in English. Followed by A or B pagination.
KpV Kritik der praktischen Vernunft (Critique of Practical Reason, 1788) Cited by page in the Akademie-Ausgabe (see Ak. below). Example: KpV 5:30 = Akademie-Ausgabe volume 5, page 30
KU Kritik der Urteilskraft (Critique of Judgment, 1790) Cited by section number (§) and Akademie-Ausgabe page. Example: KU §49 / Ak. 5:314
GMS Grundlegung zur Metaphysik der Sitten (Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, 1785) Often abbreviated in English as Groundwork or G. Cited by Akademie-Ausgabe page. Example: GMS 4:421 = Akademie-Ausgabe volume 4, page 421 (the Formula of Universal Law)
Ak. Kant’s gesammelte Schriften (Akademie-Ausgabe, Berlin, 1900–) The standard critical edition of Kant’s complete works, published by the Prussian (now Berlin-Brandenburg) Academy of Sciences. All major Kant translations print Akademie page numbers in the margins. Cited as volume:page. Example: Ak. 4:421 = volume 4 (Groundwork), page 421
GW Guyer & Wood, Cambridge Edition of the Works of Immanuel Kant The standard English-language edition of Kant, published by Cambridge University Press from 1992. Multiple volumes, each translated by different scholars under the editorship of Paul Guyer and Allen Wood. Example: GW 117 = page 117 in the relevant Guyer-Wood volume
Hegel 1770–1831
GW Gesammelte Werke (Hamburg, 1968–) The critical edition of Hegel’s works, published by the Nordrhein-Westfälische Akademie der Wissenschaften. The authoritative scholarly reference. Cited by volume:page. Example: GW 9:14 = Phenomenology of Spirit, page 14
TWA Theorie Werkausgabe, ed. E. Moldenhauer & K. M. Michel (Frankfurt, 1969–71) The 20-volume edition of Hegel most commonly used in German-language scholarship and widely accessible. Cited by volume:page.
PhS Phänomenologie des Geistes (Phenomenology of Spirit, 1807) The standard English translation is by A. V. Miller (Oxford, 1977); the Pinkard translation (Cambridge, 2018) is widely used in current scholarship. Cited by § (Miller’s paragraph numbers) or by GW/TWA page. Example: PhS §166 = Miller paragraph 166 (Master-Slave dialectic)
SL Wissenschaft der Logik (Science of Logic, 1812–16, rev. 1832) Cited by GW volume:page or by Miller translation page. The di Giovanni translation (Cambridge, 2010) is the current scholarly standard.
PR Grundlinien der Philosophie des Rechts (Philosophy of Right, 1820) Cited by § (section number, which is stable across editions and translations) or by Remark (R) and Addition (A) attached to each paragraph. Example: PR §257 = section 257 (on the state) — PR §3R = section 3, Remark
Enc. Enzyklopädie der philosophischen Wissenschaften (Encyclopedia, 1817; 3rd ed. 1830) Cited by section (§). The three parts: Logic (§§1–244), Philosophy of Nature (§§245–376), Philosophy of Spirit (§§377–577).
Schopenhauer 1788–1860
WWR The World as Will and Representation (Die Welt als Wille und Vorstellung) Vol. I (1818, rev. 1844), Vol. II (1844). Cited by volume and § (section number). The standard English translations are by E. F. J. Payne (Dover, 1969) and by Norman, Welchman & Janaway (Cambridge, 2010). Example: WWR I §57 — WWR II ch. 17
PP Parerga and Paralipomena (1851) Two volumes of essays and aphorisms. The work that made Schopenhauer famous. Cited by volume and essay/section.
Nietzsche 1844–1900
KSA Sämtliche Werke: Kritische Studienausgabe, ed. G. Colli & M. Montinari (Berlin/Munich, 1980) The standard critical edition of Nietzsche’s published works and notebooks, 15 volumes. Cited by volume:page or by work abbreviation and section number. Example: KSA 5:75 = volume 5 (Beyond Good and Evil), page 75
§ (aphorism/section no.) Section or aphorism number Nietzsche’s works are most reliably cited by section number (§), which is stable across all translations and editions. Standard abbreviations: BT (Birth of Tragedy) — HAH (Human All Too Human) — GS (Gay Science) — Z (Zarathustra) — BGE (Beyond Good and Evil) — GM (Genealogy of Morality) — TI (Twilight of the Idols) — AC (The Anti-Christ) — EH (Ecce Homo)
Kierkegaard 1813–1855
SKS Søren Kierkegaards Skrifter (Copenhagen, 1997–2013) The definitive critical edition of Kierkegaard’s complete writings in Danish, 28 volumes. Cited by volume:page. The online edition (sks.dk) is freely accessible.
KW Hong & Hong, Kierkegaard’s Writings (Princeton, 1978–2000) The standard English translation of Kierkegaard’s complete works, 26 volumes, translated and edited by Howard and Edna Hong. Each volume includes Hong’s Danish pagination in the margins. Standard work abbreviations: EO (Either/Or) — FT (Fear and Trembling) — R (Repetition) — CA (Concept of Anxiety) — CUP (Concluding Unscientific Postscript) — SUD (Sickness Unto Death)
Marx & Engels 1818–1895
MEW Marx-Engels-Werke (Berlin: Dietz Verlag, 1956–90) The standard German-language edition of the complete works of Marx and Engels, 43 volumes. The reference used in German and continental European scholarship. Cited by volume:page. Example: MEW 23:189 = volume 23 (Capital Vol. I), page 189
MECW Marx Engels Collected Works (Moscow/London/New York, 1975–2005) The standard English translation of the complete works, 50 volumes. Cited by volume:page. Example: MECW 35:189 = volume 35 (Capital Vol. I in English), page 189
MEGA Marx-Engels-Gesamtausgabe (1975–ongoing) The critical historical-critical edition of Marx and Engels’s complete writings, manuscripts, and correspondence in the original languages. Currently in progress, projected at ~120 volumes. The authoritative scholarly reference.
Capital Das Kapital, 3 vols. (1867, 1885, 1894) Cited by volume and chapter, or by MEW/MECW page. Example: Capital I, ch. 1 — Capital I MEW 23:49
IV · General Scholarly Conventions
General Latin & Scholarly Abbreviations Used across all periods
cf. confer — “compare” Directs the reader to a related passage for comparison, not as direct support.
ibid. ibidem — “in the same place” Refers to the same work and passage cited immediately before.
op. cit. opere citato — “in the work cited” Refers to a work cited earlier by the same author.
passim passim — “here and there” Indicates a concept or theme runs throughout a work, not in a single location.
trans. Translation / translated by Indicates a specific English translation is being quoted or cited.
ed. / eds. Editor / editors Indicates the editor of a collected volume or critical edition.
rev. Revised / revised edition Indicates a revised edition of a work or translation.
fr. / frg. Fragment Used for passages surviving only as quotations in other ancient authors. Example: Heraclitus fr. 30 (= DK 22 B30)
